Pride Flags and Their Meanings

Rainbow Flag-Original

The original rainbow flag. Created in 1978 by Gilbert Baker and commissioned Harvey Milk.

Rainbow Flag, 1978-1999

Flown beginning in 1978, its difference is that it is missing the hot pink stripe, which was due to a fabric shortage, and turquoise, which was omitted for color balance

Rainbow Flag

The most well-known pride flag, it is used to show that a home or business is a safe space. It omits the pink and turquoise used in Gilbert's designs for easier mass producion

Philadelphia Pride

Designed in 2017 to give representation to the unique challenges faced by the black and brown LGBT+ community

POC Pride Flag

While the origins of this particular flag are unknown, it gained prominence during the 2020 protests and represents solidarity with the Black Lives Matter movement and the distinctive intersectionality between the queer and black communities.

Progress Flag

Created as a response to the Philadelphia Pride Flag, it also adds the colors of the trans pride flag.

Progress Pride w/Intersex logo

Similar to the Progress Pride Flag, it adds the logo for intersex individuals

Straight Ally Flag

This flag is used by straight, meaning heterosexual and cisgender people who support the LGBT+ movement.

Pink Triangle Flag

Once used as a symbol by the Nazis to denote homosexual people, similar to the yellow star with Jewish people during that same time period, it now serves as a representation that history will not repeat itself.

Cisgender Flag

Cisgender means identifying as the gender one was assigned at birth

Transgender Flag

Designed in 1999, it is designed to represent those whose gender identity is not the same as their assigned birth gender.

Nonbinary Flag

Nonbinary is for those whose gender identity is beyond the gender binary (male or female).

Genderfluid Flag

People who are genderfluid don't identify with one gender, but rather their gender identity shifts between male, female, or somewhere else on the spectrum. How often someone's identity shifts depends on the individual.

Genderqueer Flag

Genderqueer people are individuals who don't conform to society's ideas of how they should act or express themselves based on the gender they were assigned at birth. According to Psychology Today, “This may be in terms of their thoughts, feelings, behaviors, and, most importantly, their gender identity.”

Lavender: Represents androgyny, White: Represents agender identities, Green: Represents non-binary people.

Agender

Agender means not idendtifying with any gender.

Intersex

Intersex is for those whose bodies do not align under the gender binary (male or female). For example, someone who was born with both sets of genitalia or has various combinations of chromosomes

Lesbian Flag

Designed in 2018, the colors represent the following:
Darkest Orange: Gender non-conformity
Middle Orange: Independence
Lightest Orange: Community
White: Unique relationships to womanhood
Lightest Pink: Serenity and peace
Middle Pink: Love and sex
Darkest Pink: Femininity

Gay Male Flag

While gay males have always been at the forefront of the gay rights movement, as the movement began to expand and diversify, the need for a flag that specifically represented gay men grew. Designed in 2019.

Bisexual Flag

Bisexuality can be defined a few different ways depending on who you ask in the community. For many, it's seen as attraction to both men and women. Others use it to describe attraction to more than one gender, but not all genders. Some even describe it as attraction to the gender you identify as and at least one other gender.

The colors represent the following:

  • Pink (or magenta): Same-sex attraction.
  • (Royal) blue: Opposite-sex attraction.
  • Purple (lavender): Attraction to both sexes.
